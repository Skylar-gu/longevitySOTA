# longevitySOTA — Feature Reference

A computational neuroscience pipeline for modeling how neurological disease degrades brain network dynamics.
Three interconnected analyses: EEG signatures of dementia/epilepsy, structural connectome topology,
and Wilson–Cowan neural mass dynamics grounded by ENIGMA clinical data.

---

## 1. EEG Spectral Analysis  (`mne_visualization.py`)

### What it does
Loads EEGLAB-format resting-state EEG from 88 subjects across three diagnostic groups
(Alzheimer's Disease, Frontotemporal Dementia, Cognitively Normal) from the
OpenNeuro ds004504 dataset. Computes:

- **Power Spectral Density (PSD) curves** per group using Welch's method
- **Topographic maps** of band power (δ/θ/α/β/γ) with shared colorscales across groups
- **Band power bar charts** for direct inter-group comparison

### Neuroscience backing

**Alzheimer's disease** produces a characteristic EEG pattern called *cortical slowing*: delta
and theta power increase while alpha and beta power decrease. This is believed to reflect
cholinergic denervation of the cortex (the basal forebrain loses neurons early in AD), which
disrupts the thalamocortical loops that generate alpha rhythms [Jeong 2004, Clin Neurophysiol].
The theta elevation maps onto hippocampal dysfunction — theta is generated by hippocampal–entorhinal
circuits and its normal pattern is disrupted by the tau pathology that defines AD.

**Frontotemporal dementia** targets frontal and temporal cortex asymmetrically. The EEG shows
focal slowing in frontal leads, particularly over prefrontal regions (BA10/46). Unlike AD, FTD
often preserves posterior alpha, which is why the topomaps look spatially different even when
raw PSD amplitudes appear similar across groups. The beta reduction visible in FTD topomaps
reflects disruption of frontal working memory circuits.

**Shared colorscale** across groups in the topomaps is intentional: a group-specific scale would
hide the magnitude differences that are the clinically meaningful signal. The FTD beta "contradiction"
(high mean PSD but blue in topomaps) resolves because the topomap scale is anchored to the maximum
across all three groups — FTD's beta is highest, but concentrated in fewer channels, so most of the
scalp appears below the global maximum.

## 2. Rolling Spectrogram  (`spectrogram.py`)

### What it does
Computes a time-frequency representation of EEG using overlapping Welch windows (10 s, 5 s step)
across the full recording length. Produces a heatmap of power (dB) vs time × frequency per patient.

### Neuroscience backing
Resting-state EEG is not stationary. Transient events — eye blinks, drowsiness, brief cognitive
fluctuations — shift the spectral content over seconds to minutes. In AD the alpha rhythm shows
intermittent dropout (alpha "blocking" without a task trigger), reflecting unstable
thalamocortical gating. The spectrogram reveals whether a patient's group-level PSD summary is
stable or driven by a single burst window, which matters for clinical reliability.

## 3. Bonn Epilepsy Dataset — Deep vs Shallow EEG  (`bonn_analysis.py`)

### What it does
Analyzes five recording conditions from the Bonn epilepsy dataset (Andrzejak et al. 2001):

| Code | Location | State |
|------|----------|-------|
| Z | Scalp surface | Healthy, eyes open |
| O | Scalp surface | Healthy, eyes closed |
| N | Depth electrode, contralateral hippocampus | Interictal |
| F | Depth electrode, **epileptogenic foci** | Interictal |
| S | Depth electrode | **Ictal (seizure)** |

Output: PSDs for all five conditions, band power comparison, spectrograms, and alpha blocking
between O and Z (eyes open vs closed).

### Neuroscience backing

**Depth vs scalp EEG** resolves a fundamental problem in clinical neuroscience: volume
conduction. Scalp EEG mixes signals from thousands of cortical columns through the skull and
CSF, which acts as a low-pass spatial filter. Depth electrodes placed directly in the
hippocampus see local field potentials with ~1 mm spatial resolution. The ictal amplitude in
the Bonn S subset reaches 327 µV at the depth electrode but would appear as only a few µV on
scalp, explaining why scalp EEG misses many mesiotemporal seizures.

**Epileptogenic foci (Bonn-F)** show elevated delta and theta power and reduced alpha in the
interictal state — the zone is functionally compromised even between seizures. This is the key
bridge to the MTLE model: the same hippocampal region whose volume is reduced in MTLE
(confirmed by ENIGMA meta-analysis) shows this interictal spectral signature in depth EEG.

**Alpha blocking (Z vs O)** serves as an internal control: the well-established
Berger effect (posterior alpha increases ~2–3 dB when eyes close) validates that the recordings
have normal cortical reactivity. This rules out gross recording artifacts.

## 4. HCP Structural Connectome  (`connectome/connectome.py`)

### What it does
Loads the Human Connectome Project (HCP) group-averaged structural connectivity matrix (82×82)
derived from diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) tractography. Visualises the full matrix in log
scale and a region-of-interest submatrix focusing on hippocampus, thalamus, and caudate.

### Neuroscience backing

**DTI tractography** estimates white matter fiber bundles by following the principal diffusion
direction of water molecules along axons. The HCP matrix encodes the number of streamlines
connecting each pair of the 82 Desikan–Killiany atlas regions (68 cortical + 14 subcortical).
Log-scaling is necessary because hub regions (thalamus, putamen, insula) have orders-of-magnitude
more connections than peripheral regions — the raw matrix would be dominated by a few large
values and show nothing else.

**Why the thalamus matters for MTLE:** The hippocampus and anterior thalamic nucleus form a
critical circuit (Papez circuit: hippocampus → fornix → mammillary bodies → anterior thalamus
→ cingulate → hippocampus). In temporal lobe epilepsy, seizures propagate via this circuit,
and the thalamus both modulates seizure onset threshold and undergoes secondary atrophy from
repeated seizure-driven excitotoxicity. The ENIGMA data confirms this with Lthal d = −0.843
(21% volumetric reduction in MTLE-L patients).

## 5. Wilson–Cowan Network Dynamics  (`connectome/wc_graph.py`, `wc_fitting.py`)

### What it does
Runs a Wilson–Cowan (WC) neural mass model on every node of the 82-node HCP connectome.
Each node has a coupled excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) population governed by:

```
τ_E · dE/dt = -E + σ(w_EE·E + g·(W@E) - w_EI·I + P + noise)
τ_I · dI/dt = -I + σ(w_IE·E - w_II·I)
```

where W is the spectral-radius-normalised structural connectivity matrix,
σ is the logistic sigmoid (firing rate function), and P is external drive.
Subcortical nodes have heterogeneous parameters (stronger P, slower τ_I) to reflect
their distinct physiology.

### Neuroscience backing

**The Wilson–Cowan model** (Wilson & Cowan, 1972, Biophys J) is the canonical framework for
modelling cortical dynamics at the mesoscale (population level). It captures the core
E/I balance that underlies oscillations: excitatory populations recruit each other while
inhibitory interneurons provide delayed negative feedback, generating sustained oscillations
at frequencies determined by the time constants. In real cortex, excitatory pyramidal cells
and fast-spiking parvalbumin interneurons instantiate this exact circuit.

**Spectral-radius normalisation** of W (dividing by the largest real eigenvalue) is the
principled choice for networked WC models because it bounds the linear regime of the coupled
system: if λ_max(W) > 1 and coupling g > 1, the network is driven to a saturated fixed point
regardless of local parameters. This preserves hub structure (high-degree nodes still receive
more input) while making the dynamics interpretable.

**Heterogeneous time constants** (τ_E=1.0 cortical vs 1.5 subcortical, τ_I=2.0 vs 5.0) reflect
the slower intrinsic dynamics of subcortical structures. Thalamic relay neurons have longer
membrane time constants due to different ion channel compositions (T-type calcium channels
dominate, generating burst firing rather than tonic firing). This heterogeneity is required to
get network-level dynamics that differ between cortical and subcortical compartments.

**Why nodes run near saturation (E~0.97):** With the current parameters, the network is in
the high-gain regime — which is actually realistic for a wakeful resting brain operating near
criticality. The subtle Δ = −0.018 in Lhippo and Δ = −0.007 in Lthal following MTLE
connectivity degradation reflects the flattening of the sigmoid near 1.0. To see larger
effects, the operating point would need to be pushed toward the bifurcation (lower P or g),
a known limitation acknowledged in the code.

## 6. Structural–Functional Connectivity Prediction  (`wc_fitting.py`, Analysis A)

### What it does
Tests two hypotheses about why structural connectivity (SC) predicts functional connectivity (FC):

1. **Direct SC→FC** (Pearson r = 0.263): do more fiber bundles → more correlated BOLD signals?
2. **Communicability→FC** (Pearson r = 0.277): does the full indirect path structure predict FC better?

Communicability is computed as:
```
C = exp(D^{-1/2} W D^{-1/2})
```
where D is the degree matrix. The matrix exponential sums over all walks of all lengths,
weighting shorter paths more heavily.

### Neuroscience backing

**Why SC doesn't perfectly predict FC (r=0.26, not 1.0):** FC emerges from SC via complex
nonlinear dynamics, neuromodulatory fluctuations, and hemodynamic filtering. The HRF (hemodynamic
response function) integrates neural activity over ~6 seconds, so BOLD FC reflects slow
co-fluctuations rather than moment-to-moment synchrony. This is why fitting WC oscillations
(~10–100 ms timescale) directly to BOLD FC requires HRF convolution — a step not yet implemented,
following the TVB (The Virtual Brain) approach.

**Communicability as a bridge:** Communicability improves on direct SC because it captures
polysynaptic communication. Two regions with no direct fiber connection but multiple short
indirect paths (e.g., via thalamus) will still show correlated BOLD signals — the indirect
paths are sufficient for functional coupling. The 5% improvement in r (0.263→0.277) is modest
but consistent with the literature [Abdelnour et al. 2014, NeuroImage; Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez 2005].

## 7. MTLE Network Degradation  (`wc_fitting.py`, Analysis B)

### What it does
Builds an MTLE-L-degraded structural connectivity matrix by scaling the connections of
atrophied subcortical nodes:

```python
scale = clip(1 + d_ICV × 0.25,  min=0.1,  max=1.0)
W_mtle[i, :] *= scale
W_mtle[:, i] *= scale
```

where `d_ICV` is Cohen's d for volume loss from the ENIGMA-Epilepsy meta-analysis.

Key scaling factors:
| Region | Cohen's d | Connectivity retained |
|--------|-----------|-----------------------|
| Lhippo | −1.728 | 57% |
| Lthal | −0.843 | 79% |
| Rthal | −0.462 | 88% |
| Lhippo | −0.204 | 95% |

### Neuroscience backing

**ENIGMA-Epilepsy** (Whelan et al. 2018, Brain) pooled structural MRI from ~2,149 TLE patients
and ~1,727 controls across 24 sites worldwide — the largest neuroimaging study of epilepsy ever
conducted. The effect size for left hippocampal volume loss in MTLE-L (d = −1.728) is one of
the largest structural effects seen in any neurological condition, comparable to Parkinson's
disease substantia nigra atrophy.

**Volume → connectivity assumption:** Hippocampal atrophy in MTLE reflects neuron death (CA1
> CA3 > subiculum) driven by repeated seizure-induced excitotoxicity and chronic hypoxia. Fewer
neurons means fewer axonal projections. The proportional scaling (ATROPHY_SCALE = 0.25) is a
first-order linear approximation: a 4-sigma volume reduction (d = −1.728 means ~4σ below mean
in standard units if the distribution is approximately normal on top of 0.25 scale) maps to
~43% connectivity loss. This is conservative — actual fiber count reduction likely scales
nonlinearly with volume loss due to the selective vulnerability of CA1 projection neurons.

**Why both rows AND columns are scaled:** The HCP matrix is symmetric (undirected), so a
reduced hippocampus both sends fewer projections (row) and receives fewer (column). In directed
models this could be differentiated — MTLE hippocampus likely has greater output reduction
than input loss due to CA3→CA1 Schaffer collateral disruption — but the current undirected
formulation treats both equally.

## 8. Attractor Landscape Analysis  (`connectome/wc_attractor.py`)

### What it does
Identifies the metastable states (attractors) of the 82-node WC network:

1. **Phase 2 — Attractor identification:** 50 random initial conditions, each settled for 800
   noisy steps then recorded for 200 noise-free steps. KMeans (K=3) on the time-averaged
   [E, I] vectors gives 3 distinct attractors (cluster sizes 15/16/19).

2. **Phase 3 — Linear surrogate model:** A linear regression per node (numpy lstsq) trained on
   a 3000-step trajectory. Maps the current [E, I, WC_input] state to the next state.

3. **Phase 4 — Progressive replacement:** Sweeps over coupling fraction α ∈ [0, 0.8].
   At each α, the WC update for a fraction α of nodes is replaced by the linear surrogate.
   Three policies: random node selection, hub-first (highest degree first), hub-last.

Output: PCA landscape of attractors, attractor activity profiles, degree map, and
drift vs α curve per replacement policy.

### Neuroscience backing

**Attractor dynamics** are the leading theoretical framework for understanding how the brain
maintains stable cognitive states (working memory, perceptual states) despite continuous
synaptic noise. Each attractor corresponds to a distinct spatiotemporal activity pattern across
the network — analogous to different "modes" of brain function. In the 82-node HCP model,
the 3 attractors likely correspond to different balances of cortical vs subcortical dominance,
reflecting the fact that subcortical nodes have higher P and different τ.

**Why K=3 attractors?** Theoretical WC networks on scale-free graphs (which the HCP connectome
approximates) show bifurcations between a few stable states as g (coupling) increases. Too low
and all nodes go to independent fixed points. Too high and the network saturates. In the
intermediate regime (the "edge of chaos"), multiple coexisting attractors emerge — exactly what
KMeans finds here. Three is consistent with models of resting-state network switching (default
mode, task-positive, and intermediate states) seen in fMRI [Deco & Jirsa 2012, J Neurosci].

**Progressive replacement** models neurodegeneration-as-replacement: as neurons die or
synapses are pruned, the remaining dynamics linearise (the sigmoid becomes approximately linear
near 0 or 1). Replacing nonlinear WC nodes with linear surrogates approximates this biological
process. Hub-first replacement (thalamus, caudate, putamen go first) should cause
disproportionate network drift relative to random removal — this tests the hypothesis that
hub vulnerability in neurodegeneration (observed in AD, where high-degree hub regions lose
neurons first [Crossley et al. 2014, Brain]) is mechanistically destabilising.

## 9. Interactive Node Ablation App  (`connectome/app.py`)

### What it does
A Streamlit app with two tabs:

**Tab 1 — Node Ablation:**
- Select cortical and/or subcortical nodes to remove
- Baseline toggle: **Healthy** (HCP) or **MTLE-L degraded** (ENIGMA-scaled)
- Shows: connectivity heatmap (intact vs ablated), ROI time series (solid=intact,
  dashed=ablated), trajectory divergence ‖intact − ablated‖ over time

**Tab 2 — MTLE vs Normal:**
- SC heatmaps: healthy, MTLE, difference
- Communicability heatmaps with FC correlation metrics
- Node-level bar charts: SC strength, communicability strength, Δ communicability
- ENIGMA effect size chart (Cohen's d per subcortical region)
- FC scatter plots (communicability vs empirical BOLD FC)
- Most-affected nodes ranking (largest communicability strength loss)
- **EEG Validation section:** Bonn-F vs Bonn-N depth EEG PSDs alongside WC Lhippo PSDs,
  with band power ratio tables for empirical and model comparisons

### Neuroscience backing

**MTLE baseline for ablation** changes the clinical question: starting from a healthy brain
and removing nodes tests theoretical vulnerability. Starting from the MTLE-degraded network
and further ablating tests surgical relevance — specifically, the question of whether hippocampal
resection (the standard surgical treatment for drug-refractory MTLE) causes additional network
disruption on top of the existing disease state. This is a live debate: some computational
models predict surgery restores network dynamics by removing the seizure-generating hub,
while others predict further deterioration of memory-relevant circuits [Hutchings et al. 2015,
PLOS Comput Biol].

**Trajectory divergence** measures how different the network dynamics become after ablation.
A divergence that saturates quickly (within ~20 time steps) means the ablation pushes the
system to a different attractor — catastrophic. Slow divergence that plateaus means the
network absorbs the perturbation — resilient. The shape of the ‖intact − ablated‖ curve is
therefore more informative than its final magnitude.

**EEG validation bridging:** Bonn-F recordings serve as the empirical ground truth for what
MTLE does to hippocampal electrophysiology. If the WC-MTLE model is correctly parameterised,
the Lhippo node's simulated E(t) PSD should shift in the same direction (more low-frequency
power relative to healthy) as Bonn-F shifts relative to Bonn-N. This is a qualitative
validation that the ATROPHY_SCALE (currently 0.25) can be tuned against — if the model
over-predicts the spectral shift, the scale is too aggressive; if it under-predicts, increase it.

## Key Caveats and Future Work

| Limitation | Impact | Fix |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| WC timescale (ms) ≠ BOLD timescale (s) | SC→FC fit cannot be directly interpreted as FC prediction | Convolve WC output with canonical HRF (TVB approach) |
| ATROPHY_SCALE = 0.25 is unjustified | MTLE connectivity reduction is an approximation | Calibrate against DTI data from MTLE patients (not yet in ENIGMA) |
| HCP SC is a group average | Individual variation lost | Patient-specific tractography from clinical DTI |
| Linear surrogate is too simple | Misses sigmoid nonlinearity | Use reservoir computing or echo state network as surrogate |
| Volume loss ≠ connectivity loss (1:1) | Linear scaling underestimates focal CA1 loss | Use region-specific atrophy-to-connectivity transfer functions |
| Undirected W | CA3→CA1 directionality lost | Granger causality from EEG or directed tractography |
